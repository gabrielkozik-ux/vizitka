AI Act for Busy People: What You Need to Know in 5 Minutes

Published: January 15, 2026 | Author: Natálie Bayerová

When clients in my law office ask about the European regulation on artificial intelligence (AI Act), most of them worry about a bureaucratic monster. The truth is that this legislation, which came into force in August 2024, fundamentally changes the rules of the game. But there's no reason to panic. Let's look at what it actually means for you as a business owner or manager, without the legal jargon, in less than five minutes.

The main goal of the regulation is to establish a safe and transparent framework. Instead of outright bans, the EU introduces rules tiered according to the danger a system poses to the health, safety, or fundamental rights of citizens. The regulation aims for balance: protecting people while maintaining room for innovation and competitiveness in the internal market.

Who It Applies To

The AI Act applies to all public and private entities that place AI systems on the EU market, or use them professionally. This is a crucial detail: the regulation applies to you even if you don't develop the software yourself but only deploy it for your employees. For average end users, virtually nothing changes in terms of obligations – on the contrary, the regulation guarantees that the technologies they interact with are safe and transparent.

How AI Works (Risk Principles)

Legislators built the entire regulation on the logic of risk profiles. Depending on how much a system can affect human lives, it is divided into four basic categories. In practice, you will encounter the following fields:

Prohibited Systems

These are technologies considered unacceptable to EU values and their deployment is banned. Examples include manipulative techniques exploiting the vulnerability of children, social credit scoring (known from China), or remote biometric identification in real-time in public spaces for law enforcement. Fortunately, you will rarely encounter these systems in ordinary business.

High-Risk Systems

These systems are permitted but subject to the strictest obligations throughout their lifecycle. This covers data governance, documentation, human oversight, and cybersecurity. Typically, these are systems in critical infrastructure, like train traffic management or energy distribution. In everyday practice, however, businesses most often encounter high-risk systems in HR – for instance, algorithms for automated screening and sorting of applicant resumes. Here, many managers fail to realize that buying such software shifts compliance responsibility to their shoulders.

Limited Risk Systems

This covers most generative models we use daily for work – ChatGPT, Google Gemini, or deepfake creation tools. The main requirement here is transparency. The user must clearly know they are interacting with an AI or consuming synthetic content.

Minimal Risk Systems

The final, residual category poses virtually no danger to society and remains unregulated. This includes common spam filters in your email inboxes or AI behavior of opponents in video games.

In practice, this means that for most businesses, the AI Act will not mean an immediate administrative storm. If you don't develop software yourself and only integrate it for internal needs, the bulk of the obligations lies on the shoulders of developers and providers of these technologies.

However, I recommend not waiting with folded arms for 2027, when full effectiveness takes place. The first step should be an internal audit – map out what tools your people are actually using and what data they upload into them. You will protect your sensitive know-how much sooner than the legislation forces you to.
