"AI Slop" Before the Czech Supreme Administrative Court: Two Lessons from One Judgment

Published: July 17, 2026 | Author: Mgr. Gabriel Kožík

On 1 July 2026, the Czech Supreme Administrative Court (SAC) issued a judgment (case no. 10 As 81/2026-27) that would have been routine — had the court not paused over the "quality" of the cassation complaint and used a term we had not encountered in a Czech judicial decision before: "AI slop"[1]. The judgment offers two valuable lessons: one for anyone who owns property or runs a business, and one for us professionals who work with artificial intelligence.

The case: a tram line and an "omitted" owner

The owner of a plot neighbouring a tram line modernisation project in Litvínov challenged the building permit in court, claiming he knew nothing about the proceedings — nobody had invited him or served him anything. The regional court dismissed the action for failure to exhaust ordinary remedies, and the SAC agreed.

The catch: the case was a proceeding with a large number of parties (more than 30). In such proceedings the law allows — and for strategic infrastructure directly prescribes — service on secondary parties by public notice: the document is posted on the official notice board and is deemed served on the fifteenth day. Parties are identified not by name but by their cadastral plot numbers. The owner was therefore not omitted at all — he was duly identified by his plot number and everything was served on him. He simply never read it.

Lesson one: what nobody mailed you still binds you

This is the point every property owner and business should know. In large proceedings, the responsibility for keeping up with official documents shifts to you. The authority has no duty to write to your mailbox — the notice board, including the electronic one, is enough. Miss your appeal because of it and you lose judicial protection too: judicial review only comes after administrative remedies are exhausted.

In this case the authority even made a mistake — it failed to advise parties served by public notice about the right to appeal. Even that did not save the owner: where the advice is missing, an extended deadline applies, but no more than 90 days from notification. That, too, expired unused.

The practical takeaway: if you own property near planned infrastructure, monitor the official notice boards of the relevant authorities — electronically it takes minutes a month. By the time the decision "arrives", it will be too late. It already arrived.

Lesson two: a court names "AI slop" for the first time

Now the part that has lawyers passing the judgment around. The SAC observed that a substantial part of the cassation complaint was not a genuine argument with the lower court's reasoning but "text apparently generated by artificial intelligence (AI), which the attorney did not take much trouble to revise". And the court took the trouble to dissect it:

Of the cited decisions meant to support the argument, five had nothing to do with the issue at hand. One judgment carried a wrong date. Another did not exist at all. And a regional court decision was passed off as an SAC judgment. That pattern — confidently cited but non-existent or irrelevant case law — is the signature of unedited AI output.

The SAC firmly urged the attorney to avoid such "careless practice" in the future and reiterated that professionals bear full responsibility for their submissions — including any hallucinated AI argumentation. It referred to its own judgment case no. 3 As 34/2025-80 and to the Constitutional Court's decision no. I. ÚS 3004/25, which fined an attorney CZK 25,000 for a similar submission — covered in my article AI in Court: Five Rulings Every Business Owner Should Know. Czech case law on AI in court filings is starting to build up nicely.

What to take away

Both lessons share one denominator: responsibility cannot be delegated — not to an authority, and not to an algorithm. A property owner is responsible for watching what officially happens around his assets. A professional is responsible for every word he signs, no matter who — or what — wrote it. AI can speed up research, but verifying every citation against the actual source is the non-negotiable minimum. That is exactly how I work with AI, and exactly what I teach companies: the tool may be fast, but the guarantee must be human.
