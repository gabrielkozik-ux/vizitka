Mandatory socks with sandals, by law?

Published: July 23, 2026 | Author: Mgr. Gabriel Kožík

It sounds like an absolute absurdity, but Czech public television used exactly this example on the show What the Fact? to brilliantly and clearly break down how the Czech legislative process actually works. I've been watching the show for a while, and after seeing this episode I knew I had to share it.

I'd recommend it to every business owner. It does a great job demystifying how the rules you have to play by in business actually come into being.

Note: the video is in Czech and covers the Czech legislative process specifically. The three takeaways below apply just as well if you do business in Czechia, regardless of which language you follow the news in.

Why should this matter to you? Because hidden inside that process are traps that can blindside your business overnight.

Czech Television illustrates the process using a fictional bill that would make wearing socks with sandals mandatory.

Here's my summary of the three things every business owner should take away from the episode:

The standard route vs. the parliamentary shortcut

When the government proposes a bill, it passes through civil servants, experts and the Legislative Council of the Government – including an inter-ministerial comment procedure and a Regulatory Impact Assessment (RIA). That takes months.

But there's also the "member's bill" – a legislative express lane that skips those brakes entirely. Nobody systematically checks the impact, or even whether the reasoning behind it makes sense. That's often exactly how the kind of confusion is born that later gives you a headache.

How laws quietly "bloat"

In the first reading, debate is often waved off with "we'll sort out the details later." Then the bill drops into the committees of the Chamber of Deputies and moves to the second reading – and that's where the real fun begins. New clauses get added, along with exemptions (say, for children under 12, or a tax break for medical compression socks), and, crucially, penalties. A simple original idea balloons, and in practice you're left guessing what you actually have to comply with.

But watch out – this risk isn't limited to member's bills. Riders can attach themselves to a carefully prepared government bill just as easily, once it reaches committee.

The Senate, the President, and even the Constitutional Court aren't a universal safety net

A lot of people live under the illusion that if something truly foolish makes it through, there's a bulletproof safety net in the Senate, the President, or the Constitutional Court. In reality, the Chamber of Deputies can override a Senate rejection or a presidential veto with an absolute majority (101 votes out of all deputies). And the Constitutional Court, when it does review a law, doesn't ask whether it's foolish, illogical, or pushed through by lobbying – it asks whether it conflicts with the constitutional order. A law can be foolish or cunning and still be perfectly constitutional.

The exception is extreme cases where a law is arbitrary or undermines legal certainty to such a degree that this alone makes it unconstitutional – but that's a last-resort brake, not an insurance policy against every badly drafted provision.

What does this mean for your business?

The Czech rule of law relies on procedure and on the good faith of the people involved. When that good faith runs out, procedural loopholes let things through that you never would have imagined at the outset.

So what can you practically do about it? Keep an eye on legislative developments in your industry. Ideally, you want to be ready for new legislation well before it's on your doorstep.
