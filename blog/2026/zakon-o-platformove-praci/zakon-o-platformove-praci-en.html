The End of Fines for People and the Rise of Algorithms: How the New Czech Legislation Will Change (Not Only) Platform Work and the Schwarz-system

Published: June 8, 2026 | Author: Mgr. Gabriel Kožík

At the EU level, the Digital Omnibus has postponed the effectiveness of rules for high-risk AI systems to December 2027.

However, the Czech Republic will likely regulate automated task assignment, performance evaluation, human oversight, and other rights and obligations arising from the use of so-called algorithmic management—which is expected to be largely driven by AI—as early as January 2027.

So, what should employers prepare for? Who will this "platform law" and related changes affect, and what does it all mean?

Myth: "It Only Affects Uber and Wolt"

When the term "platform work act" is mentioned, most Czech managers and entrepreneurs wave it off, thinking they don't deliver food or run a taxi service. But this is a big mistake. The definition of a digital labor platform in the draft act is extremely broad.

It covers any natural or legal person who provides a commercial service at a distance by electronic means (website, mobile application) and organizes work performed by individuals.

Do you run a local cleaning company with an app? A dispatch system for tradespeople? A portal for external designers or translators? If your software organizes people in any way—for example, by assigning jobs based on ratings, location, or speed, determining prices, or penalizing rejections—you are a platform under this law.

What's more: accompanying changes in the Labor Code will impact all employers in the Czech Republic who use modern technologies to manage people, regardless of whether they qualify as a platform.

1. When People Are Managed by Code Instead of a Manager (Algorithmic Management and AI)

If your company falls under the definition of a platform, you will face obligations stemming from so-called technological compliance. The law distinguishes between automated monitoring systems (which electronically track and evaluate people) and automated decision-making systems (which automatically assign tasks, change compensation, or block accounts).

What are you newly forbidden to do, and what, on the contrary, must you fulfill?

  • Ban on digital surveillance. Algorithms must not analyze the mental, emotional, or health status of workers, read their private chats, or track them outside of working hours.
  • Human-in-the-loop. If an account is to be blocked, compensation reduced, or a contract terminated—meaning any decision with significant impact on the rights and obligations of the affected persons—the decision must not be made by software. The algorithm can only propose the decision. The final word must belong to a human. Furthermore, the worker has the right to immediate written justification and review. You must then prove how the decision-making process was set up.
  • End of information asymmetry. Workers and their representatives must know exactly what parameters the algorithms track, what data they collect, and how this data affects their compensation or evaluation. They must receive this information no later than their first day of work.
  • Right to data portability. Workers obtain the right to export their performance and evaluation data in a structured format, enabling them to easily switch to a competing platform.

2. Psychosocial OHS – A New Standard for All Employers

In this regard, the legislation also applies to all other employers, such as standard offices, call centers, warehouses, or production facilities and other.

The amendment to the Labor Code introduces an explicit obligation for employers to identify and assess psychosocial and ergonomic risks associated with algorithmic management.

If you use software for productivity monitoring or shift scheduling, you must prove that the system does not exert undue pressure on people and does not threaten their mental health (e.g., through unrealistically generated time limits). Additionally, you will have to discuss the introduction of such systems in advance with employee representatives.

3. Schwarz-system: The End of Fines for Workers and a Procedural Trap for Employers

The state's fight against the "schwarz-system" (disguised employment) has historically run into the fact that fines threatened both parties—the employer and the self-employed contractor. This is set to change starting January 2027. The law completely abolishes the offense of performing illegal work for the worker. All risk and liability will be concentrated entirely on the "quasi-employer". This brings two new risks for companies.

The draft platform act establishes that if the labor inspection detects undeclared dependent work, a legal presumption arises that a valid employment relationship exists between the company and the worker.

If you terminate cooperation with a worker and they sue you claiming it was retaliation for exercising their new rights (e.g., requesting information about the algorithm), you as the company will bear the burden of proof in court to demonstrate that this was not the case. For departing, dissatisfied contractors, this opens up an exceptionally strong negotiating position.

How to navigate this? Three steps you must take this year.

Although the rules will only become effective at the turn of 2026 and 2027, resetting internal processes, software, and contractual documentation will take months. I recommend starting with the following.

  1. Conduct an audit of B2B relationships. Do not rely on what is written in the contract. The Labor Code newly defines the relationship of superiority and subordination purely by whether you organize and supervise the work during working hours. Review the actual reality of cooperation with your (sub)contractors.
  2. Review your internal systems. If you use software to allocate work and track performance, map out what data you collect. You will need to prepare a so-called data protection impact assessment (DPIA) and prepare documents for consultation with employees.
  3. Embed "human-in-the-loop" into your processes. If your systems already handle compensation, selection of suitable contractors for individual tasks, or potential terminations of cooperation, a human element must be verifiably embedded in this chain. A human must have the final word.
