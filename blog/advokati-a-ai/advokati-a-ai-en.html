The Legal Profession at a Crossroads: Threat, Tool, or Future?

Published: October 6, 2025 | Author: Mgr. Gabriel Kožík

Artificial intelligence (AI) is no longer a topic of science fiction but a tangible reality permeating all professional fields. The traditionally conservative legal profession is no exception. Are lawyers endangered? This article aims not to provide a simple answer but to analyze whether this technological shift represents an existential threat to Czech lawyers or rather an unprecedented opportunity for transformation and growth.

The Dichotomy of Artificial Intelligence in Law: Tool vs. Threat

Understanding the impact of AI on legal practice requires recognizing its fundamental duality. It is not a purely positive or negative technology; its influence is determined by how it is used and the degree of critical thinking with which its outputs are accepted.

AI as an Accelerator of Efficiency and Quality: The PROs

The main argument for introducing AI into the legal profession is its ability to dramatically increase efficiency and free up human potential for tasks requiring higher cognitive skills.

  • Automation of routine tasks: Legal research, case law analysis, contract review, and drafting initial document versions.
  • Increased productivity: Freeing up capacity for higher value-added work – strategy, negotiation, and client communication.
  • Enhanced quality and predictive analysis: Detecting inconsistencies, reducing the risk of errors, and predicting dispute outcomes based on data.

Risks and Ethical Challenges: The CONs

Uncritical adoption of AI is associated with serious risks that can threaten not only individual cases but the integrity of the entire profession.

  • Factual unreliability and "hallucinations": AI can invent facts, cite non-existent case law, or refer to repealed laws.
  • Lack of contextual and emotional understanding: AI lacks human judgment, empathy, and the ability to understand the subtle nuances of human relationships.
  • Threat to professional duties: Inputting sensitive information into public models directly conflicts with the duty of confidentiality. Moreover, the lawyer, not the machine, is always fully responsible for the advice given.
Artificial intelligence will not replace humans. But people who use it will replace those who do not.

Practical Impacts of AI on Legal Practice in the Czech Republic

Most Czech and Slovak law firms are already using artificial intelligence in some form. Specialized tools (Lawly, Advomate) are emerging on the market, changing daily work and the structure of the legal market.

  • Threat to junior positions: Automation most significantly affects tasks traditionally performed by junior lawyers and paralegals.
  • Strengthening the role of senior lawyers: The role of seniors is shifting towards supervision, strategic planning, and final review of AI outputs.
  • Changing business models: The pressure for efficiency is forcing firms to move from hourly rates to project-based pricing or value-based billing.

Regulatory and Ethical Framework in the Czech Republic

The Czech Bar Association (ČAK) has issued an opinion emphasizing that AI is merely a tool and that the lawyer always bears full responsibility. The duty of confidentiality is an absolute priority. At the European level, the Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act) introduces a risk-based approach. Most tools in the legal field will fall into the minimal-risk category, where transparency will be key.

Risk Description in the Legal Context Mitigation Strategies and Recommended Practices
Factual Errors and "Hallucinations" Generating incorrect legal information, citing non-existent case law. Never copy without verification; use specialized legal tools; implement a two-phase review process.
Breach of Attorney-Client Privilege Inputting sensitive client data into public AI models. Prohibit the use of public AI for client data; use secure/private instances; anonymize data.
Loss of Critical Skills Over-reliance on AI can lead to the atrophy of analytical and research skills. Educate lawyers to see AI as an assistant, not a substitute; adapt training models.
Legal and Ethical Responsibility Ambiguity regarding liability for AI errors. According to professional regulations, it is always the lawyer. Establish clear internal guidelines; verify professional liability insurance; maintain documentation and an audit trail.

Conclusion: How to Adapt and Thrive?

Passive waiting is not a sustainable strategy. The future of law lies in a "centaur" model – a symbiosis of human intuition and strategic thinking that directs the immense computational power of AI. The successful lawyer of the future will not be the one who tries to beat AI, but the one who learns to "ride" it best.

  • For individuals: Proactively self-educate, develop "human" skills (critical thinking, empathy), and build a personal brand.
  • For law firms: Develop an AI strategy, invest in secure technologies and training, and revise business models.
  • For educational institutions: Modernize curricula to include legal technology and data literacy.
